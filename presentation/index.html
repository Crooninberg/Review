Sentinels with Dubstep (Skrillex)

  • Sound-based project using songbirds as a model learning system
  • Canaries are treated as a sentinel species, historically used to detect invisible environmental risk
  • Birds are exposed to bursty, high-contrast sound patterns resembling electronic music structures
  • Focuses on timing, uncertainty, and repetition rather than musical genre or preference
  • Draws parallels between acoustic training, game-like reinforcement, and digital media environments
  • Examines how learning systems adapt to variable reward and prediction error
  • Frames sound as an optimized signal that shapes behavior through feedback loops
  • Positions birds as early-warning sensors for the gamification of learning and attention

Description

By training canaries on dubstep-like patterns, the project uses songbirds as sentinels to expose the same game-like reinforcement dynamics that structure video games and digital media.

Doxologies

A video game controlled not by hands, but by brain activity (noninvasive brain–computer interface).

Read more

This work extends the investigation of gamified learning by constructing a full video game controlled not by hands, but by brain activity. Using a noninvasive brain–computer interface, neural signals such as attention, relaxation, or oscillatory patterns are translated into in-game movement and decision-making. The player does not issue commands directly; instead, the system responds to measurable states of the nervous system.

By shifting control from intentional action to neural modulation, the game exposes how feedback loops operate below conscious choice. The player learns not through strategy alone, but through regulating internal states in response to variable reinforcement. Success emerges from synchronization rather than mastery.

Positioned alongside earlier work with songbirds trained on bursty sound structures, the game frames the human nervous system as another learning system embedded in a gamified environment. Where birds adapt to acoustic patterns, the player adapts to a system that rewards specific neural dynamics. In both cases, behavior is shaped by timing, uncertainty, and feedback rather than content or narrative.

The work asks what it means to play a game when the interface is the nervous system itself — and what kinds of selves are produced when attention, arousal, and focus become inputs to optimized systems.

Previous Work

Skinsuits

  • Infinite game environment cycling through thousands of avatars across video game and media worlds
  • Avatars are procedurally generated and continuously reassigned
  • Operates via a playable character or by mapping avatars directly onto an audience member’s body
  • Uses motion capture and computer vision with large-scale projection
  • Identity shifts in real time through gesture and movement
  • Explores the Proteus effect, where behavior and self-perception adapt to avatar form
  • Reveals how posture, confidence, and presence change with representation
  • Examines identity as a temporary interface shaped by game systems rather than choice
Read more

This project is an infinite game environment built around rapid avatar transformation. The player—or an audience member whose body is mapped in real time—moves through thousands of playable identities drawn from across video game and media universes, from Mario and Zelda to Silent Hill and Grand Theft Auto. Avatars are procedurally generated and endlessly recombined, allowing the system to run indefinitely.

Using motion capture and computer vision, the work can operate either through a virtual character or by projecting avatars directly onto a participant’s body at full scale. As gestures are mapped onto constantly shifting forms, the participant’s posture, movement, and behavior subtly change in response to each identity.

The project engages the Proteus effect -the phenomenon in which a person’s behavior and self-perception shift to align with the characteristics of their avatar. By accelerating and destabilizing avatar identity, the work reveals how quickly embodiment, confidence, aggression, vulnerability, or restraint can be altered by representation alone.

Rather than asking who a player chooses to be, the work asks what happens when identity is continuously reassigned—when the body becomes a surface for endless projection, and the self adapts in real time to the logic of game systems.
